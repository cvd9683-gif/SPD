01Overview

A familiar website that no longer worked for its catalog.

The Society of Publication Designers is a community of art directors, designers, illustrators, and photographers working in editorial. Their website holds decades of competition winners, recurring events, member-facing content, and organizational pages — but the structure underneath had grown faster than it had been organized.

This case study walks through the redesign in detail: how we explored the problem, what we found, what we chose to focus on, and where the project still has open questions.

02Problem & Setup

What was wrong — and how we explored it.

The problems below were visible from the existing site. Visible isn't the same as understood. To know what to redesign, we had to know why the existing structure produced these problems — three methods of investigation followed.

Before redesigning the surface, redesign the structure underneath it. — Project framing, Website Redesign Team

  • 01
    Navigation didn't scale with the catalog.
    Top-level labels were broad and inconsistent. From any single entry point, it was hard to surface the breadth of competitions, events, and resources the org actually offers.
  • 02
    Pages were dense and hard to scan.
    Membership and benefits content ran long, with little internal structure — making it hard to compare options or skim for the relevant detail.
  • 03
    Information hierarchy was unclear.
    Primary tasks like joining or entering a competition competed for visual weight with secondary content, slowing decisions on every page.
  • 04
    Discovery was limited.
    Moving laterally between related competitions, winners, and resources required backtracking through the navigation rather than following clear connections within the content.
  • 05
    Visual presentation didn't match SPD's editorial caliber.
    For an organization that recognizes excellence in editorial design, the existing site read as functional rather than design-led — undermining credibility before content was read.

03Insights

What the research actually told us.

Each insight came directly from one of the methods above. The artifact alongside it is the source — included so the reasoning can be traced back, not taken on trust.

01

The catalog isn't oversized — it's under-organized.

From the audit: most pages cluster around a small set of recurring types — competition, winner, event, resource, member tier. Each had been built individually. The work isn't to cut content; it's to reduce the number of templates that content lives in.

This single reframe carried more weight than any other in the project. Once we treated it as a templating problem, every later decision became easier.

Stakeholder synthesis

02

The site is being read as a record, not a tool.

This was the clearest gap between internal and external mental models. Stakeholders described the site as documenting SPD's history; visitors described it as something they were trying to use. Neither reading is wrong — but only one of them was being designed for.

The redesign has to do both. But "tool" needs to lead, because that's the unmet need.

Source — Stakeholder interviews
03

Membership is a comparison, not a narrative.

The current page reads as a single long-form article. The decision visitors are making is structurally a comparison — which tier? — and the page wasn't doing the comparing for them.

Once we treated it as a comparison, the structure (and most of the layout decisions) followed directly: side-by-side first, long-form context after, one CTA per tier instead of several.

Source — Audit + user interviews
04

Restraint is the unusual move in this category.

Among peers, most sites lean visually busy — heavy hero treatments, multiple competing modules, dense type. A more disciplined system stands out, and signals competence to an audience that evaluates editorial work professionally.

Source — Competitor scan
Peer scan
Peer landscape · 8 sites

Editorial-design and adjacent organizations, sampled for how each handles long-running competitions, events, and membership.

05

Lateral movement matters more than top-level nav.

Many of the visitor paths the audit and task analysis surfaced were lateral: competition → winner → next year's competition → related resource. The existing site routed all of those through the top-level menu.

Connecting content directly to content was a higher-leverage change than rewriting labels — it solves the underlying movement, not the symptom of it.

04Focus & Direction

The turning point: choosing what not to do.

We chose to focus on structure, not surface.

+ What we kept in scope

  • Reorganize navigation around tasks. The single highest-leverage move. Nav by task, not by department.
  • Design five recurring page templates. Homepage, competition, winner, event, member tier — every other page inherits.
  • Restructure membership as a comparison. Match the layout to the underlying decision (which tier?), not the content type (long article).
  • Add lateral content connections. Winner ↔ competition, year ↔ year, resource ↔ topic — within the content, not via the menu.
  • Establish a restrained visual system. One condensed display family, one body family, one accent — SPD red.

− What we dropped or deferred

  • A full content rewrite. Out of scope this cycle. Editorial follow-up handed to the SPD team with a content guideline.
  • Custom per-competition styling. Replaced by the competition template. Year-specific identity now lives in the content (imagery, copy), not the structure.
  • A new search feature. The audit suggested nav-level fixes would close most of the search use cases. Search becomes a follow-up if data shows otherwise.
  • A members-only portal. Out of scope. Worth revisiting once the public site stabilizes — different problem with different requirements.
  • An immediate visual rebrand. The system extends SPD's existing identity rather than replacing it. A rebrand is a separate project, not a side-effect.

Every direction shift came from the same realization — the site's surface problems were structural, not visual. Restyling without restructuring would have produced a prettier version of the same site. — Project framing, post-research

Decision 03 · Lateral

Connections live in the content, not the menu.

The existing site forced visitors back through the top-level nav for related content. The redesign builds those connections directly into the templates — fewer trips through the menu, fewer dead-ends.

Competition ↔ Winner A competition page surfaces its winners directly. Each winner links back to the competition that produced it — closes the most-walked lateral path on the existing site.
Winner ↔ Year archive Winners link sideways to neighboring years and categories. Browsing across years no longer requires returning to the menu.
Resource ↔ Topic / event Resources connect to related events and competitions by topic — keeps content discoverable beyond its origin section.
Member tier → Competition entry Membership pages link forward into the tasks members do most. Reduces the gap between considering joining and using membership.

Flow 01 · Homepage

From "everything at once" to "one job at a time."

The existing homepage was carrying identity, current-cycle promotion, archive entry, and navigation in one screen. The redesign reduces that to one statement of identity, three task entry points, and a single editorial strip below the fold.

Sketch → v1
Tested whether the homepage could survive being this small. v1 confirmed structure on its own, without visual treatment doing the work.
v1 → refined
Added the editorial strip after feedback that the page felt "too quiet" — addressed without losing the focus the v1 had earned.
Tradeoff
Less real estate to promote any single moment from the homepage. Mitigated by the strip, which the team can re-program each cycle without redesigning the page.
On the refined wireframe
  • 1
    Identity-led hero. One clear statement of what SPD is.
  • 2
    Three primary actions. Drawn from the highest-intent paths in the audit.
  • 3
    Editorial strip. Replaces the previous module-stack — single, scannable row.

Flow 02 · Competitions & Winners

From annual one-offs to a single template.

Each annual competition had its own page; winners were spread across pages with inconsistent layouts. The redesign collapses this into one template per content type, with shared filters and direct lateral links.

Sketch → v1
Tested a single repeating card pattern across all years and categories. v1 added the persistent filter rail, replacing the dropdown nav the existing site used.
v1 → refined
Moved lateral links (winner ↔ competition) into the content itself, rather than routing them through the menu as v1 still partially did.
Tradeoff
Loses some bespoke styling that flagship years had. Gains consistency, comparability, and substantially less per-cycle design effort going forward.
On the refined wireframe
  • 1
    Persistent filter rail. Year, category, and medium stay visible while scrolling.
  • 2
    Cards anchored on the work. The piece itself leads — surrounding metadata is secondary.
  • 3
    Lateral links. Winner ↔ competition connections live in the content, not the menu.

Flow 03 · Membership

From long-form article to side-by-side comparison.

The existing membership page presented all tier information, benefits, and FAQs as continuous prose. The redesign reframes it around the underlying decision — which tier? — placing the comparison up front and keeping context after.

Sketch → v1
Reframed the page around the decision, not the content. v1 added anchored sub-navigation so visitors could jump rather than scroll.
v1 → refined
Reduced each tier to a single CTA. Stakeholder review showed multiple CTAs per tier were the largest source of friction in v1.
Tradeoff
Visitors who want the long-form context have to scroll past the comparison to reach it. Accepted — far more visitors are comparing than reading.
On the refined wireframe
  • 1
    Anchored sub-nav. Lets visitors jump to the relevant section.
  • 2
    Tier comparison. Side-by-side, not stacked — matches the decision shape.
  • 3
    One CTA per tier. Removes the previous page's stacked-options noise.
07Reflection

What worked, what didn't, what we'd improve.

Quantitative outcomes will be added once the site has been live long enough to measure against the existing baseline. Below: an honest accounting of where the project stands, written before those numbers come in.

What workedReframing nav around tasks unlocked the rest.

The single highest-leverage decision was reorganizing the navigation around what visitors come to do, not how SPD is internally structured. Every later decision — templates, hierarchy, which content to surface — fell out of that one reframe more easily than expected.

The second was treating recurring page types as templates instead of pages. That move came directly from the audit, and it's what makes the redesign maintainable rather than a one-time facelift.

What didn'tSome decisions were made on too little evidence.

User interviews were still in progress when several layout decisions were locked in. The choices we made hold up against the audit and stakeholder conversations, but a few of them — particularly around the homepage editorial strip and the membership comparison module — are running ahead of direct user input.

If those interviews surface something we didn't expect, the templates can absorb it. But the honest version is: the redesign is more confident in some areas than the evidence currently supports.

What we'd improveEditorial discipline needs its own system.

Strong components only carry a site so far. The team's ongoing ability to write task-led copy and curate the homepage strip is what will keep the redesign coherent six months in — and that wasn't a primary deliverable this cycle.

Next time, I'd build the content guidelines alongside the design system, so editorial discipline is captured the same way the visual one is. That's the place the redesign is most exposed to drift.
